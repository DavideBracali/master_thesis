\subsubsection{Epileptic seizures}

Epilepsy is a chronic noncommunicable neurological disorder characterized as 
the recurrence of unprovoked seizures, which are brief episodes of abnormal
electrical activity in the brain.
Epileptic seizures can result in symptoms such as involuntary movement,
convulsions and loss of consciousness \cite{WHO2016Epilepsy}.

Epilepsy is one of the most common neurological diseases globally: it is
estimated that approximately 50 million people suffer from epilepsy, and
about $5\%$ of people will have experienced at least an unprovoked seizure by
the age of 80 \cite{ep-wilden}.
Approximately $69\%$ of the cases can be controlled using anti-seizure
medications, as drug-resistence is a frequent complication \cite{ep-eadie}.

Seizures consist of the disruption of the normal balance between 
excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission,
Resulting in abnormal, excessive and synchronized neuronal activity.
Epileptic seizures are a highly heterogeneous phenomena.
They can arise from a variety of underlying conditions, and in approximately
$50\%$ of cases, the cause remains unknown \cite{WHO2016Epilepsy}.
Significant variability exists in the type, severity and anatomical origin of a
seizure.
Such differences are present both between different patients and between
different seizures of the same patients \cite{ep-pennell}.  

\subsubsection{Seizure Onset Zone (SOZ)}

Epileptic seizure are distinguished in two major types: generalized seizures
and focal seizures.
Generalized seizures involve rapid and near-simultaneous abnormal electrical
activity across both cerebral hemispheres, without a stable onset zone. 
Focal seizures initially present electrical symptoms in a specific region of
the brain, called Seizure Onset Zone (SOZ).
Once initiated the seizure may spread to adjacent areas, involving a vaster 
area of the brain.

At seizure onset, neurons exhibit a transition from normal asynchronous activity
to excessive and synchronized firing.
Seizure activity may manifest as high frequencies oscillations, synchronized
coupling or transient events such as spikes and sharp waves.
These patterns can vary between patients or between different seizures of the 
same patient, but consistently present a transition between normal and 
epileptogenic state.
The SOZ is defined by the brain regions that present the earliest detectable
deviations from the normal background activity of neurons.
