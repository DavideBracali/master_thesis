\section{Results and Discussion}    

\subsection{Local features importance}   \label{res:importance}

In this section, we analyze the onset dynamics on a subset of 4 patients.
This analysis was performed in order to define the most discriminative 
local features to differentiate between contacts that were labeled as SOZ
and healthy regions of the brain.

Because this investigation was performed preliminarily and defined design
choices in the model pipeline, we decided to exclude these 4 patients from the 
model evaluation to prevent any potential information leakage that would lead to
an overestimation of the model performances.
They appear as part of the training set in the hyperparameter optimization, but
are never included in neither validation and test set.

A two-sided Mann-Whitney test was performed on the distributions of each of the 
22 local features during the onset of the seizure, defined as a $[t_\text{onset}
-10\ s, t_\text{onset} + 20\ s]$ interval.
Both rank biserial correlation (whose definition is reported in Section
\ref{meth:opt:vaegmm}) and p-value are reported, to measure effect size and
statistical significance.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Immagini/feature_effect_size.png}
    \caption{Discriminative power of local features in a subset of 4 patients.}
    \label{fig:effect_size}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:effect_size} displays how betweenness centrality has both the 
largest effect size and the only significant p-value.
Equivalently, it is the only local feature whose distribution in the seizure
onset is significantly different between SOZ and non-SOZ labeled contacts.

This result directly influences the architecture of the alarm classifier
described in Section \ref{meth:classifier}, as the negative log-likelihood is 
weighted according to the absolute value of the Z-score of betweenness
centrality.
However, the proposed model does not solely rely on betweenness centrality, but
considers a negative log-likelihood score that reflects the anomalies 
across the joint distribution of all different features.
Betweenness centrality is only used, together with the temporal index, as a 
weighting factor for anomalies, amplifying those occurring when the most
discriminative feature shows strong deviations.

\subsection{A classification example}    \label{res:example}

To present the behavior of the proposed model, we present a classification 
example on a representative patient.
This patient was selected as it produced one of the clearest classification 
examples in the analyzed subset.
In this case study we present the training and anomaly detection process in
the latent space, showing how local feature deviations translate into alarm
scores.  

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Immagini/example/DE/tr_lanent_space.png}
    \caption{Latent space of resting-state activity.}
    \label{fig:exDE:tr}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:exDE:tr} displays the VAE encoding of the resting-state local 
features of training patients.
Partial overlap between different patients is visible, indicating that the VAE
can successfully encode similarities between the brain activity of different 
patients.
However, regions of the latent space predominantly occupied by a specific
patient exist, indicating different resting state dynamics across patients.
This behavior can be interpreted as a consequence of the great heterogeneity in 
EEG recordings: common patterns between patients exist, but the differences in
individual brain activity result in patient-specific regions of the latent space.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Immagini/example/DE/tr_gmm.png}
    \caption{GMM fit of resting-state latent distribution.}
    \label{fig:exDE:tr_gmm}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:exDE:tr_gmm}, displaying the GMM fit on the training distribution,
shows that a moderate number of low-likelihood outliers is present in the training 
set, indicating that even resting-state activity can show anomalous behavior.
These outliers may reflect atypical behavioral or cognitive states occurring
during resting-state recordings, as patients are not under strict experimental
control and may engage in voluntary activities that alter their neural dynamics.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Immagini/example/DE/tr_gmm_test.png}
    \caption{VAE encoding of the seizure onset on GMM fit of resting-state features.}
    \label{fig:exDE:tr_gmm_test}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:exDE:tr_gmm_test} shows how features are encoded in the latent
space during the $[t_\text{onset} - 10, t_\text{onset} + 20]$ time interval.
Red dots represent the contacts labeled as SOZ, while green dots correspond to
non-SOZ contacts.
As expected, both SOZ and non-SOZ contacts present a mix of typical and 
anomalous behavior during the seizure.
The SOZ may exhibit typical activity before or after the onset, and 
healthy regions will show epileptogenic behavior during the transition from
localized to general features.
This plot shows that the log-likelihood distribution of the encoded features 
alone cannot determine which contacts contribute to the seizure onset, but its
evolution in time must be considered as well.

Figure \ref{fig:exDE:neg_ll} plots the negative log-likelihood time series of
each contact.
This time series serves as input for the alarm classifier, and can be used to
visually assess how well each contact conforms to the resting-state distribution
during the seizure: most of the contacts that compose the SOZ light up
a few seconds before the generalized seizure, identifiable by the simultaneous
and widespread anomalies.
Gaussian smoothing reduces noise while preserving prolonged anomalies,
maintaining meaningful information while discarding isolated peaks.

The negative log-likelihood time series is then processed through the alarm
classifier, that produces a continuous alarm score that is thresholded to
obtain a binary classification.
The ranking of the top 100 suspicious contacts is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:exDE:alarm}, where red bars represent contacts labeled as 
epileptogenic, blue bars indicate non-SOZ contacts and the dashed
horizontal line is the classification threshold.

\subsection{Optimization Results}       \label{res:opt}

Hyperparameter optimization was performed as described in Section \ref{meth:opt}.
In this section we will present the final parameters,
the distribution of the best parameters for the cross-validation,
the learning curves, and the distributions of the optimization target for
training, validation and test set.
The results presented in this section will be discussed in Section
\ref{res:discussion}.

\subsubsection{VAE and GMM}         \label{res:opt:vaegmm}

Optimization of the VAE and GMM architecture was performed for 300 iterations on
each LOOCV fold.
Each iteration proposes a new set of parameters for the VAE and GMM
architecture, training the model on the candidate parameters.
To limit computation time, the VAE and GMM were trained on a uniformly sampled 
subset of 200 seconds of temporal observations drawn from the training
patients.
Each temporal observation includes the resting-state features of all contacts
for the corresponding training patient at the specified time index.

As discussed in Section \ref{meth:opt} the validation target\footnote{
Rank biserial correlation of one-sided Mann-Whitney test comparing the negative
log-likelihood of SOZ and non-SOZ contacts during a seizure. Refer to Section
\ref{meth:opt:vaegmm} for details.}
on each fold was monitored during training.
The median validation target stopped increasing after iteration 193,
for this reason the final training before evaluating test patients was limited
to 193 iterations.

Figure \ref{fig:opt1_params} shows the best parameters for each LOOCV fold,
while Table \ref{tab:opt1_params} presents the best parameters returned by the
final training procedure.
The learning process of the final training is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:opt1_learn}, while Figure \ref{fig:opt1_target} displays the 
target distribution on training, validation and test set.

\subsubsection{Classifier}      \label{res:opt:classifier}

Optimization of the alarm classifier was performed for 200 iterations on
each LOOCV fold.
Preliminarily to the classifier optimization, VAE and GMM are trained on a
uniformly sampled subset of 2000 seconds of temporal observations drawn from 
the training patients, using the best parameters reported in Table
\ref{tab:opt1_params}.
At each iteration a new set of parameters is proposed to the classifier, that
receives as input the negative log-likelihood returned by the optimized VAE and
GMM.

The median validation target\footnote{
Arithmetic average of precision (PPV) and specificity (SPC) multiplied by a
penalty function. Refer to Section \ref{meth:opt:classifier} for details.}
stopped increasing after iteration 173,
therefore the final training was limited to 173 iterations.

Figure \ref{fig:opt2_params} shows the best parameters for each LOOCV fold,

\subsection{Discussion}         \label{res:discussion}

Although promising results were obtained during cross-validation, it is evident 
that the proposed model failed to generalize to a subset of unseen patients:
Figure \ref{fig:prec} and Figure \ref{fig:spec} show that the 
model obtained worse precision and specificity distributions on test patients
compared to those observed in the training and validation sets.
While all validation metrics distributions significantly differ from the ones
produced by the random classifier, the same cannot be said for test
distributions\footnote{
Given the limited number of independent test patients ($N = 4$), the statistical 
power of the Mann–Whitney test is inherently low. Therefore, the lack of 
statistical significance should not be interpreted as evidence of equivalent
performance.
Nevertheless, the performance drop observed on the test set indicates that the
model overfits training data and struggles to generalize to unseen patients.}.

This result is likely to be a consequence of the combination of two factors:
a limited number of patients and a great heterogeneity  in seizure and 
resting-state dynamics.
In the LOOCV setting, the selected hyperparameters are highly sensitive to the
specific training fold, as
the replacement of a single patient in the training set leads to substantially
different optimal hyperparameters (Figure \ref{fig:opt1_params} and Figure
\ref{fig:opt2_params} in Section \ref{res:opt}).
This result is reinforced by the fact that the VAE encoding of resting-state 
activity presents high variability between different patients (Figure
\ref{fig:exDE:tr} in Section \ref{res:example}).
As a consequence, training target distributions during optimization present
very high variance (Figure \ref{fig:opt1_target} and Figure
\ref{fig:opt2_target}), as the model struggles to
find a unique set of parameters to obtain acceptable performances on all
patients.

It is also worth noticing the instability of the VAE and GMM optimization
learning curve in Figure \ref{fig:opt1_learn}, indicative that the model
performance is highly sensitive to small variations of the parameters.
Together with the fold-dependent sensitivity observed in LOOCV, this further
explains the limited generalization capabilities on unseen patients.

Validation performances, on the other hand, appear comparable with the training
set.
However it should be noted that the number of training iterations before 
evaluation of the test set was selected based on the validation performance
itself.
Because of the low number of patients, both training and validation set are not
representative for the test set.
Therefore validation performances only represent an optimistic estimate of the 
real generalization capabilities of the model.

The VAE optimization also resulted in a surprising parameter choice: Table
\ref{tab:opt1_params} reports that the optimal number of hidden layers as
$L=0$, both for the final training and across the majority of training folds 
during cross-validation. 
This result is unexpected, as it indicates that the latent space is built by
sampling from a normal distribution whose mean and log-variance are built
from a linear combination of the resting-state features.
The stochastic sampling in the latent space still introduces non-linearity,
however the best encoder model is essentially a linear perceptron.
Optimization may prefer an almost-linear model to obtain acceptable overall
performance on all training patients, whereas a more complex model would
memorize noisy patterns and lose generalization capabilities in such a diverse
dataset.

The automated version of Bartolomei et al. Epileptogenicity Index was proven
incapable to return acceptable classification performances on both validation 
and test patients.
After tuning the two hyperparameters that define the algorithm on the training set,
null precision (Figure \ref{fig:prec}) was obtained test patients and the majority
of validation patients.
Equivalently, no True Positives were classified for both validation and test
patients.
Its high specificity (Figure \ref{fig:spec}) values suggest that the model only
classifies a few contacts as positive, as a large fraction of non-SOZ
contacts is rightfully classified as negative.

\subsection{Future developments}              \label{res:future}

The proposed pipeline provides an initial approach to the generalization of 
the SOZ localization in multiple patients.
However, several critical points have emerged from the results, suggesting
possible directions for improvement.

A moderately low number of patients was available for this study and,
as discussed in Section \ref{res:discussion}, the dataset exhibits high
inter-patient heterogeneity .
As a consequence, all metrics reported in Section \ref{res:eval} highlight that
the model struggles to find a unique set of parameters to generalize to many 
patients, obtaining low performances on test patients and high-variance 
distributions on training and validation patients.
Because of the low sample size, design choices according the validation set 
result in significantly lower test performances, as training and validation
set are not representative for unseen patients.

A greater patient availability may improve generalization on test patients.
However it would probably not be sufficient to reduce the variance of encoded
data, as training the model with a moderate number of patients proved to result 
in a sparse latent space and variable performance.
To achieve so, patient specific fine-tuning could be employed in the training
process:
after an initial training phase using all the other patients, the pre-trained
VAE adjusts its weights using the resting-state activity of the patient to be
evaluated as input.
This technique could adapt the encoded distribution to be patient specific,
potentially reducing its variance and improve anomaly detection,
however optimization would become much more time consuming:
because training must be performed on each training patients, computation
time would scale linearly with the number of training patients during
optimization.

Another suboptimal result is that the VAE optimization converged to a model
with no hidden layers, preferring a linear encoder and decoder architecture
in the majority of training folds:
The current implementation does not fully exploit its non-linear feature
extraction potential.
For this reason, future work might explore different models to perform
dimensionality reduction.
Linear models could be tested, for example Principal Component Analysis (PCA,
unsupervised) or Partial Least Square (PLS, supervised) regression.
Additionally, the choice of using a GMM to fit the encoded distribution should
not be intended as unique, but different density estimation methods could be
tested.
In a large-scale study on this topic, every step of the pipeline should be
compared against its possible alternatives in order to understand their
strengths and limitations.

The decision to perform a two-step hyperparameter optimization is also a
limitation itself:
it reduces the dimensionality of the parameters space reducing the number of
iterations required for convergence and improving stability, but makes the
final results heavily dependent on the choice of the intermediate target. 
Different objective functions could be experimented in order to achieve
optimal results, for example minimizing the VAE validation loss.
